Introduction to Digital Humanities

What is Digital Humanities?

Digital humanities work is done at the intersection of computational methods and humanities materials. Let’s break down this definition and explore what it means in practice.

Key Definition

Digital humanities: The application of computational methods to humanities research questions and materials.

Digital humanities projects typically involve three main components:

  1. Materials (e.g. texts, images, artifacts)
  2. Processing (computational analysis)
  3. Presentation (results display)

The Digital Humanities Workflow

The fundamental activities in digital humanities projects typically include:

  1. Mediation/remediation
  2. Datafication/modeling
  3. Processing/analytics
  4. Presentation/display
  5. Sustainability/preservation

Let’s explore each of these in more detail.

Mediation/Remediation

This involves converting analog materials into digital formats, or working with born-digital materials.

Important

Remember that the process of digitization itself involves decisions that can affect research outcomes.

Datafication/Modeling

This is the process of abstracting discrete values from phenomena or artifacts. It involves decisions about what can be measured and how.

Processing/Analytics

This stage involves using computational methods to analyze the data. Common techniques include:

  • Text analysis
  • Network analysis
  • Geospatial analysis

Presentation/Display

Results are often presented through:

  • Visualizations
  • Interactive websites
  • Digital exhibits

Sustainability/Preservation

Considerations for long-term access and maintenance of digital projects.

Example 1: Text Analysis of Literary Works

library(pacman)
p_load(tidyverse,tidytext,wordcloud,gutenbergr)

# Function to download and process text
 gutenberg_works(title == "The Age of Innocence")
# A tibble: 1 × 8
  gutenberg_id title     author gutenberg_author_id language gutenberg_bookshelf
         <int> <chr>     <chr>                <int> <chr>    <chr>              
1          541 The Age … Whart…                 104 en       Movie Books/Bestse…
# ℹ 2 more variables: rights <chr>, has_text <lgl>
 inno <- gutenberg_download(541)

# Analyze word frequencies
word_freq <- inno %>%
  unnest_tokens(word, text) %>%
  anti_join(stop_words) %>%
  count(word, sort = TRUE) %>%
  top_n(20, n)

# Create a bar plot of word frequencies
p <- ggplot(word_freq, aes(x = reorder(word, n), y = n)) +
  geom_col() +
  coord_flip() +
  labs(x = "Word", y = "Frequency", 
       title = "Top 20 Most Frequent Words in The Age of Innocence",
       subtitle = "After removing common stop words") +
  theme_minimal()

# Print the plot
print(p)

This word cloud visualizes the most frequent words in The Age of Innocence, giving us a quick insight into common themes and vocabulary.

Example 2: Sentiment Analysis of Jane Austen’s Novels

library(janeaustenr)

# Combine Austen's novels
austen_books <- austen_books() %>%
  group_by(book) %>%
  mutate(linenumber = row_number(),
         chapter = cumsum(str_detect(text, regex("^chapter [\\divxlc]", 
                                                 ignore_case = TRUE)))) %>%
  ungroup()

# Perform sentiment analysis
austen_sentiment <- austen_books %>%
  unnest_tokens(word, text) %>%
  inner_join(get_sentiments("bing")) %>%
  count(book, index = linenumber %/% 80, sentiment) %>%
  pivot_wider(names_from = sentiment, values_from = n, values_fill = 0) %>% 
  mutate(sentiment = positive - negative)

# Plot sentiment over narrative time
ggplot(austen_sentiment, aes(index, sentiment, fill = book)) +
  geom_col(show.legend = FALSE) +
  facet_wrap(~book, ncol = 2, scales = "free_x") +
  labs(title = "Sentiment Analysis of Jane Austen's Novels",
       x = "Narrative Time", y = "Sentiment") +
  theme_minimal()

This visualization shows the emotional trajectory of Jane Austen’s novels over their narrative time.

  2. How might this type of analysis enhance our understanding of narrative structure?

Example 3: Network Analysis of Historical Figures

library(igraph)
library(ggraph)

# Create a sample dataset of historical figures and their connections
historical_figures <- tibble(
  from = c("Napoleon", "Napoleon", "Wellington", "Nelson", "Wellington", "Nelson"),
  to = c("Wellington", "Nelson", "Nelson", "Napoleon", "Louis XVIII", "Emma Hamilton")
)

# Create and plot the network
set.seed(2023)
historical_figures %>%
  graph_from_data_frame() %>%
  ggraph(layout = "fr") +
  geom_edge_link(aes(edge_alpha = 0.5)) +
  geom_node_point(color = "lightblue", size = 10) +
  geom_node_text(aes(label = name), vjust = 1.5, hjust = 0.5) +
  theme_void() +
  labs(title = "Network of Historical Figures")

This network visualization shows connections between some key historical figures of the Napoleonic era.

Challenges and Considerations in Digital Humanities

Ethical Considerations

Digital humanities projects often involve ethical considerations, particularly when working with sensitive or personal data.

Warning

Always consider privacy, consent, and potential harm when working with data about individuals or communities.

Interdisciplinary Collaboration

Digital humanities often requires collaboration between humanists, computer scientists, librarians, and others.

Discussion: Collaboration Challenges (10 minutes)

Critical Approaches

It’s important to maintain critical perspectives on the use of digital tools in humanities research.

Note

Remember that computational methods are not neutral. They embed certain assumptions and biases that need to be critically examined.

Conclusion

These demonstrations showcase just a few of the exciting possibilities that digital humanities offers for exploring and analyzing humanities data. By combining computational methods with traditional humanities scholarship, we can uncover new patterns, ask novel questions, and gain fresh insights into cultural and historical materials.
